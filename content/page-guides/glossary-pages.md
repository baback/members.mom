# Writing Guide: Glossary Pages (`/glossary/[slug]`)

## Overview

These are top-of-funnel informational pages targeting people searching "what is [term]". The reader is learning — they might be a junior marketer, a brand owner new to video, or someone researching the space. Your job is to give the clearest, most useful definition on the internet, then connect the term to the product naturally.

## Tone & Voice

- Write like a smart teacher — clear, patient, no condescension
- Explain concepts simply but don't dumb them down. Assume the reader is intelligent but unfamiliar with this specific term
- Use analogies and real-world examples to make abstract concepts concrete
- Authoritative but approachable — this is a reference page, not a blog post
- No marketing language in the definition sections. Save the product tie-in for its own section

## Total Word Count

**800–1,200 words** per page. These are concise reference pages, not long-form guides. Quality over quantity.

## Section-by-Section Breakdown

### 1. H1 + Meta
- Format: "What Is [Term]? Definition, Examples & How It Works"
- Meta description (155 chars): Start with a clean definition, end with "Learn how it works with examples."
- Example: "UGC (user-generated content) is content created by real people, not brands. Learn what UGC means for marketing, with examples and how AI is changing it."

### 2. Definition Box (30–50 words)
- A visually highlighted box (bordered, shaded background) at the very top
- 2 sentences maximum. Crystal clear.
- Format: "[Term] is [definition]. It's used in [context] to [purpose]."
- Example: "**UGC (User-Generated Content)** is content — videos, photos, reviews — created by real people rather than brands. In marketing, UGC is used in ads and social media because it feels authentic and builds trust."
- **This box should be good enough to be a featured snippet on Google**

### 3. Why [Term] Matters (150–200 words)
- 3 reasons why this term is relevant to marketers/brands
- Each reason: bold label + 2 sentences
- Connect the term to business outcomes (revenue, cost, efficiency) — not just theory
- Example for "Thumb-Stop Rate":
  - **It predicts ad performance**: "If people don't stop scrolling, nothing else matters. Thumb-stop rate is the first filter your ad has to pass."
  - **It's measurable**: "Unlike 'brand awareness,' you can track thumb-stop rate in your ad dashboard and optimize for it."
  - **It guides creative decisions**: "A low thumb-stop rate means your hook isn't working — change the first 1–2 seconds, not the whole ad."
- This section should make the reader think "I need to pay attention to this"

### 4. How It Works (250–350 words)
- Practical explanation with examples
- Use subheadings if the concept has multiple components
- Include at least 1 concrete example or scenario:
  - "For example, if your video ad has a thumb-stop rate of 25%, that means 1 in 4 people who see your ad in their feed actually stop to watch it. The industry benchmark for TikTok is 15–25%, so 25% is solid."
- If the term involves a formula, show it: "ROAS = Revenue from Ads ÷ Cost of Ads. If you spent $1,000 and made $4,000, your ROAS is 4.0x."
- If the term involves a process, use numbered steps
- Use analogies for abstract concepts: "Think of hook rate like the subject line of an email — it determines whether anyone reads the rest."
- **Don't over-explain. If you can say it in 250 words, don't stretch to 350.**

### 5. How ReUGC Helps With [Term] (100–150 words)
- Soft product tie-in — NOT a sales pitch
- Connect the term to a specific product capability
- Format: "If [term] matters to your marketing, here's how ReUGC helps:"
- 2–3 bullet points, each connecting the concept to a feature:
  - "**Test hooks at scale**: Create 10 video variations with different hooks in minutes, so you can find the highest thumb-stop rate without spending weeks on production."
  - "**AI actors, no scheduling**: Generate testimonial-style videos on demand — no creator coordination, no delays."
- Keep it helpful, not pushy. The reader should think "oh, that's a smart way to apply this concept" — not "ah, here's the sales pitch."

### 6. Related Terms (50–80 words)
- List 4–6 related glossary terms with internal links
- Format: "**Related terms:** [Hook Rate](/glossary/hook-rate), [Creative Testing](/glossary/creative-testing), [Creative Fatigue](/glossary/creative-fatigue), [CTR](/glossary/ctr)"
- Add 1 sentence explaining the relationship: "Thumb-stop rate is closely related to hook rate — thumb-stop measures who stops scrolling, while hook rate measures who keeps watching past the first 3 seconds."
- This section is critical for internal linking and topical authority

### 7. CTA Section
- Standard CTA component
- Keep it simple: "See how ReUGC helps with [term]."
- Button: "Get Started"

## Formatting Rules

- Definition box must be the FIRST thing after the H1 — no intro paragraph before it
- Use H2 for main sections only (Why It Matters, How It Works, etc.)
- Formulas should be in code blocks or highlighted boxes
- Related terms should all be internal links
- Keep paragraphs short — 2–3 sentences max. These are reference pages.
- Add schema markup: FAQPage or DefinedTerm schema for rich snippets

## What NOT to Do

- Don't write a 200-word intro before the definition. Definition first, always
- Don't make the definition box longer than 2 sentences. If you need more, put it in "How It Works"
- Don't turn the product section into a sales page. 1 short section, 2–3 bullets, done
- Don't use circular definitions ("UGC is content that is user-generated")
- Don't skip the example in "How It Works" — abstract definitions without examples are useless
- Don't link to external competitors in glossary pages — keep all links internal
- Don't pad these pages to 2,000 words. 800–1,200 is the sweet spot. Short and authoritative beats long and fluffy
